Web Archiving Projects

Chelsea Cates

Below is a list of art museums, libraries, professional groups and other organizations that have created collections of archived digital resources. Most of these institutions have a current web archiving program and are consistently updating their collections. This list is by no means comprehensive, but is meant to show the range of art resources on the web being collected today.

The Art Libraries Society of North America Artist Files Special Interest Group (SIG) began a web archiving project in 2012 to preserve the born-digital items normally collected by art libraries in artist files. Although the program is no longer active, the collections are still accessible.

The Artist Files SIG partnered with Archive-It, the web archiving service offered by the Internet Archive, to collect a specific group of web pages. Archive-It was especially interested in the Artist Files SIG project because it required their crawlers to capture different types of files. Archive-It donated server space and a data budget to the group which created two collections: Contemporary Art Galleries and Contemporary Art Galleries 2.

These two collections contain a wide range of sites including regional gallery websites, university web pages, and institutional and private blogs. As a result, the Archive-It crawlers captured:

  • Exhibition checklists and installation images
  • Press releases and exhibition reviews
  • Artist biographies and interviews
  • Gallery publications
  • Event announcements

The collections also includes some complex web elements like embedded video, image carousels, and interactive tours.

Metadata was assigned at the document, or web page, level and includes Library of Congress subject headings, in addition to gallery and artist names, in order to make the collections easy to search.

To learn more about the ARLIS/NA Artist Files SIG web archiving project see the article “The Future of Artist Files: Here Today, Gone Tomorrow” by Samantha Deutch and Sally McKay in the Spring 2016 issue of Art Documentation.

Archive Highlight

L.A. Louver
L.A. Louver is a contemporary art gallery, established in 1975, in Los Angeles, California. The December 13, 2014 capture, screencapped below, collected an interactive tour of the gallery.

The National Museum of Women in the Arts is located in Washington, D.C. and is the only museum in the world dedicated to collecting and exhibiting women artists. Their permanent collection contains the art work of over 1,000 women and they present five to eight special exhibitions each year. These temporary exhibitions feature the work of women from around the globe.

The NMWA’s web archiving program began in 2011 and uses Archive-It, a web archiving service offered by the Internet Archive, to capture over 100 websites in three categories:

  • Contemporary Women Artists on the Web
  • NMWA Websites
  • Online Exhibitions

The program collects the websites of contemporary women artists, women-run artist organizations, and artist profiles on art gallery sites. It archives the NMWA's institutional websites and social media, including their blog, Flickr, Facebook, Twitter, and the Clara Database of Women Artists. The NMWA also archives their own web-based exhibitions.

Archive Highlight

Clara Database of Women Artists
The Clara database is a interactive resource with information on over 18,000 women artists taken from the NMWA archive.
The database is no longer being updated.

The New Museum is New York City's only museum dedicated to contemporary art. The musuem's web archiving program uses the Archive-It service, offered by the Internet Archive, to capture their own website as well as born-digital press about the museum and its exhibitions. The New Museum has also captured several of their web-based projects since the web archiving program began in 2013.

Archive Highlight

Your Land / My Land
"Your Land / My Land: Election ‘12" by Jonathan Horowitz is an installation work created around the 2012 election that took place simultaneously at seven museums across the United States. This webpage, able to straddle all seven physical sites, is a record of the multiple, unique events.

The New York Art Resources Consortium is made up of the research libraries of the Brooklyn Museum, the Frick Collection, and the Museum of Modern Art. Together, they run an ambitious web archiving program to collect, preserve, and provide access to a curated collection of websites that complement each library’s print holdings. Their collections also include the archived websites of each member institution.

The NYARC web resources program collects 3,934 websites in the following ten categories:

  • Art Resources
  • Artists’ Websites
  • Auction Houses
  • Brooklyn Museum
  • Catalogues Raisonnés
  • Museum of Modern Art 
  • New York Art Resources Consortium (NYARC)
  • New York Galleries
  • Restitution of Lost and Looted Art
  • The Frick Collection

For its web resources program, NYARC uses Archive-It, the web archiving service offered by the Internet Archive. Archive-It crawls the live web and collects specific web pages using URLs pre-determined by NYARC.

NYARC has been web archiving since 2010 but began to collect most of their sites in 2014. They are committed to documenting their web archiving practices and maintain a wiki on the subject.

Archive Highlight

The Medieval Bestiary
The Medieval Bestiary, established in 2002 and updated until 2011, is a rich resource for exploring medieval manuscripts, bestiaries, and the iconographical significance of specific animals. Users can search by bibliography, beast, or manuscript.

Since 2013, the Sterling and Francine Clark Art Institute Library has used the Archive-It web archiving service, provided by the Internet Archive, to collect web pages associated with the Venice Biennale. The Venice Biennale is an international art exhibition, held in Venice, Italy every two years, where invited artists show work alongside installations from different countries. Many collateral art events also take place in the city while the exhibition is open. The Biennale’s unique and temporary nature make it the perfect subject for web archiving.

Every Biennale year the Clark Art Institute Library creates a new collection and begins archiving sites when the international exhibition opens in the spring. They stop archiving in November when the Biennale closes. Documents captured include:

  • Blogs
  • Artist videos
  • Participant websites
  • Official Biennale websites
  • Websites for collateral events
  • Social Media

In 2013, the first year of the web archiving project, 99 sites were preserved. In 2015 there were 206 sites captured and in 2017 the Clark Art Institute Library collected 175 sites.

Archive Highlight

Helen Sear... The Rest is Smoke
Helen Sear was invited by the United Kingdom to represent Wales at the 2015 Venice Biennale. The archived webpage of her installation "The Rest is Smoke" includes a video which shows site-specific projections, as well as a brief interview with Sear and the exhibition curator Stuart Cameron.

Rhizome is a born-digital organization found online, and at the New Museum, that supports born-digital works of art through exhibitions, commissions, preservation, and software development.

Rhizome uses Webrecorder, a program the organization designed in-house, to record born-digital web-based artworks. These works are then made accesible at webenact. Currently webenact provides access to 21 works of art and one blog which contain interactive elements and embedded video that can be difficult for a web crawler to capture.

In addition to Webrecorder, Rhizome has developed several other open source web archiving tools.
