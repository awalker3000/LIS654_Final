Why Archive?

We all understand the need to preserve and archive important paper documents, but we may not realize it is equally important to preserve and archive digital documents, especially those on the web. Websites change, move, and disappear entirely, making important content inaccessible and previously used content unverifiable.

Web archiving is the process of making copies of these endangered web pages from the live web and recording information about them, especially the date on which they were copied. This allows the webpage to be stored, preserved, and accessed in the future.

Increasingly, art librarians are tasked with preserving born digital and digitized art history resources such as:

  • Museum and Gallery websites
  • Contemporary Artist websites
  • Web-based exhibition catalogues, auction catalogues, and catalogues raisonné
  • Institutional records of exhibitions, symposia, workshops, and other events
  • Online art history resources used and cited by scholars
  • Correspondence between artists, scholars, and patrons that exist online or over social media
  • Any other online resource in danger of moving or disappearing

Archiving these materials will make is possible for future researchers to access important information about the art world today, even if the original assets disappear from the live web.

Take contemporary art gallery websites for instance. These galleries don’t produce catalogues for each exhibition they mount. Instead, they feature the work currently on view on their website, including images and object information. Galleries often include work from past exhibitions and artist profiles and past work on their websites as well. It is vital to preserve and make accessible gallery websites for future art historians as they are essential source of information about today’s art world that does not exist in any other format.
